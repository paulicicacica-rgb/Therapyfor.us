A grief that gets minimized

When a friendship ends—whether through distance, betrayal, or death—something breaks that the world doesn't have language for. Your best friend was the person you texted at 11 p.m. The one who knew your fears before you spoke them. The one who made you laugh when nothing else could. But when they're gone, people ask about your romantic relationships, your family. They don't ask about them. And the silence makes it worse.

You might hear: "You can make new friends." Or "They're still in your life, just differently." These words, meant kindly, land like dismissals. They make you wonder if you're overreacting. If your grief is too big for what people think should hurt. It isn't. Losing your best friend is losing a version of yourself—the self that existed in their presence, with their laughter, their belief in you, their knowing glance across a crowded room.

I felt like nobody actually understood that losing my best friend was losing my person. Therapy gave me permission to grieve that fully, without apology.

The weight of this kind of loss sits differently in your chest. It's not just sadness—it's identity confusion, resentment, sometimes rage at being left behind or let down. You replay conversations. You second-guess whether you could have done more, said something different, been better. And underneath it all is the bone-deep loneliness of not having the one person who would help you through hard things. A therapist can sit with all of that without trying to fix it or speed it up.

Why this hurts so much—and why you need support

Friendship loss is grief, plain and simple. But it's grief wrapped in cultural invisibility. There's no formal mourning ritual. Nobody brings you casseroles. Your workplace doesn't give you bereavement days. So you carry it alone, showing up as if nothing changed, while everything feels hollowed out. Over time, this can deepen into depression, isolation, or a persistent numbness that makes other relationships harder to sustain.

A therapist trained in grief and loss helps you name what happened. They validate that this matters. They teach you how to process not just the absence, but the betrayal, anger, regret, or shame that often tangles up with friendship grief. And they help you rebuild—not by replacing this person (impossible), but by understanding who you are on the other side of losing them.

What helps

Therapy for friendship grief creates a judgment-free space where your loss is taken seriously. Your therapist helps you untangle complicated emotions, process what was gained and lost, and gradually build meaning from the pain—without rushing you toward closure.

Questions people ask before starting

Is it normal to be this devastated over a friendship? Shouldn't I be over it by now?
Yes. Grief doesn't have a timeline, and it doesn't care about social hierarchy. A best friend is often your chosen family, your safe person. Losing that is a legitimate loss, and it can take months or years to process. There's no 'should' here.
Will a therapist think I'm overreacting or judge me for how angry I feel?
No. A good therapist meets you where you are without judgment. They've helped many people through friendship loss and understand that anger, despair, and confusion are all normal parts of grief. You won't be rushed or minimized.
How much does therapy cost, and how often would I need to go?
Most people start with weekly sessions at around $60-90 per week through BetterHelp, and you'll get 20% off your first month. Many find weekly sessions helpful for grief work, though your therapist will help you figure out what's right for you.
Can therapy actually help if the friendship is already gone?
Absolutely. Therapy doesn't bring the friendship back—it helps you process the loss, understand what happened, rebuild your sense of self, and move forward without being trapped in the grief. People often find unexpected healing once they start.
What if I find the wrong therapist and it makes things worse?
You can switch therapists anytime, at no penalty. Finding the right fit matters, especially with grief. If the chemistry isn't there in the first session or two, just try someone else. Your comfort comes first.
If you are in crisis or having thoughts of harming yourself, call or text 988 immediately — the Suicide and Crisis Lifeline, available 24 hours a day in English and Spanish. BetterHelp is not a crisis service.
