The unspoken exhaustion of raising kids alone

You wake up before them. You're the one who knows their schedule, their fears, their favorite foods. You're the provider, the homework helper, the referee between siblings, the person who has to be strong even when everything feels impossible. There's no tag-out. No one else checking if the bills are paid or if their soccer uniform is clean. And at night, when they're asleep, the silence hits differently.

The hardest part? Nobody really talks about it. Your friends don't ask how you're actually doing. Your family might offer help but not understanding. You're expected to handle it—and you do, because what choice do you have? But handling it and thriving are completely different things. You might be managing, but you're not okay. And admitting that feels like failure.

I was drowning but keeping my head above water so nobody would notice. Therapy gave me permission to admit I was struggling—and that wasn't weakness, it was finally getting real help.

The isolation is its own weight. You're juggling work, parenting, logistics, and emotional labor all at once. There's little room for your own thoughts, your own grief, your own anger. Maybe you're grieving the family structure you imagined. Maybe you're carrying resentment. Maybe you're just tired—so tired that you can't remember what you want anymore, only what the kids need. That matters. Your exhaustion is not a character flaw. It's evidence that you need support.

Why this weight feels so heavy—and why talking helps

Single parenting rewires your nervous system. You're always on. Always responsible. Always the decision-maker. There's no backup, which means your body stays in a low-level emergency state—even when everything is technically fine. Over time, that builds into anxiety, burnout, resentment, or depression. You might snap at your kids over small things. You might feel numb. You might lie awake worrying about money or whether you're messing them up. These aren't signs you're failing. They're signs you need a space to process what you're carrying.

Therapy gives you that space. It's not about fixing your situation—you're already managing that. It's about processing the emotional weight, building tools to manage stress, and reconnecting with yourself as a person, not just a parent. A therapist won't judge you for struggling. They won't tell you to just toughen up. They'll help you understand what's driving your exhaustion and give you actual ways to protect your mental health while you're in the thick of it.

What helps

Therapy for single dads works because it meets you where you are—no judgment, no platitudes. You get to talk about the real stuff: the loneliness, the financial pressure, the fear you're not enough. That processing, combined with real coping strategies, lightens the load in ways that nothing else can.

Questions people ask before starting

Won't therapy cost more money I don't have?
Online therapy through BetterHelp costs as little as $70-90 per week—comparable to a couple of dinners out—and fits into your schedule without adding drive time. We offer 20% off your first month. Many people find it's one of the few things they invest in themselves, and it pays back in better sleep, less anxiety, and healthier parenting.
I don't have time for therapy. I barely have time to breathe.
That's exactly why it matters. Sessions are 45 minutes once a week—you schedule them when it works for you, even after the kids are in bed. Many single dads say it's the only time they have to think about themselves, and that actually makes them better at everything else.
Will a therapist judge me for struggling?
No. Therapists work with people in hard situations every day. They're not there to tell you you're doing it wrong. They're there to help you understand your stress, give you tools that actually work, and remind you that struggling doesn't mean failing.
What if therapy doesn't actually help?
It's not magic, but research shows that talk therapy significantly reduces anxiety and depression—especially when you find the right fit. If your therapist isn't working for you, you can switch anytime, at no cost. The goal is finding someone who gets it.
What if I don't know where to start or what to say?
Your therapist will guide you. You don't need a perfect story or all the answers prepared. Start with what brought you here: the overwhelm, the exhaustion, the weight. That's enough. They'll help you go from there.
If you are in crisis or having thoughts of harming yourself, call or text 988 immediately — the Suicide and Crisis Lifeline, available 24 hours a day in English and Spanish. BetterHelp is not a crisis service.
