The strange quiet of feeling nothing

You're not depressed in the way movies show it. You're not crying or spiraling. Instead, there's just... absence. You go through the day like you're watching it happen to someone else. Good news arrives and you feel a distant sort of 'okay.' Bad things happen and you can't muster the energy to care. It's like someone turned down the volume on everything—your emotions, your desires, your sense of being alive.

This isn't laziness. It's not that you're broken. Emotional numbness is your mind's way of protecting itself when it's been overwhelmed, stressed, hurt, or worn down. Your nervous system has essentially pulled a circuit breaker. The problem is, that protection eventually becomes its own prison.

I used to love photography. Now I have a camera I don't touch and I feel nothing when I look at old photos. I know I should feel *something*, but there's just this blank space where the joy used to be.

What makes this harder to name is that you might look fine on the outside. You're functioning. You're getting things done. But inside, you're aware that something is missing—that gap between how you feel and how you think you should feel. That awareness itself becomes painful, even if you can't quite access the feeling underneath it.

Why numbness happens—and why therapy actually helps

Emotional numbness often shows up after prolonged stress, trauma, chronic anxiety, grief, or burnout. Sometimes there's no single trigger—it just accumulates. Your brain is trying to survive by turning down the intensity dial, but what starts as survival becomes disconnection. You lose touch not just with pain, but with pleasure, connection, motivation, and meaning.

Therapy doesn't force feelings back. Instead, a good therapist helps you understand why your system went numb in the first place, what it's protecting you from, and how to gently rebuild your emotional capacity. This happens through talking, through being witnessed, through learning what your body needs. Many people find that within weeks of consistent therapy, the color starts coming back—slowly at first, then more fully. You don't have to feel broken forever.

What helps

Emotional numbness responds well to therapy because it's not a character flaw—it's a nervous system response. A therapist trained in this can help you understand the root cause, rebuild safety in your body, and reconnect with feeling. Online therapy makes this accessible without the friction of finding someone locally or waiting months for an appointment.

Questions people ask before starting

Will a therapist just try to make me feel things I'm not ready to feel?
No. Good therapy works at your pace. Your therapist's job is to help you understand why you're numb and create safety in your nervous system first. Feelings come back naturally once that foundation is there. You're always in control.
What if I start therapy and realize I can't actually feel anything—even with help?
Emotional numbness is one of the most treatable patterns in therapy. Most people notice shifts within 4-6 weeks of consistent work. If something isn't working with your therapist, you can switch anytime—no penalty, no explanation needed.
How much does online therapy cost, and can I afford it weekly?
BetterHelp starts at $90-100 per week for unlimited messaging with your therapist, plus weekly video sessions. New members get 20% off their first month. Many people find it's less than in-person therapy and fits better into their schedule.
Will talking about my numbness actually change anything?
Research shows that having a space to name what you're experiencing—without judgment—is actually the first step toward change. Your brain needs to feel safe and understood before it can thaw. That's exactly what therapy provides.
What if I get a therapist I don't click with?
You can switch therapists anytime for free. BetterHelp makes it easy to find someone who fits you better. The relationship with your therapist matters, so don't settle for someone who doesn't feel right.
