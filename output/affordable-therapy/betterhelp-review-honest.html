Why you're researching this in the first place

The real strengths—and real limits—of BetterHelp

Let's start with what BetterHelp does genuinely well. Their therapist roster is massive (over 30,000 licensed counselors), which means shorter wait times and more chances of finding someone who fits your specific needs and background. The platform itself is smooth: messaging between sessions is included, you can switch therapists without penalty, and you're not locked into a contract. For someone with a chaotic schedule, severe anxiety about in-person appointments, or who lives somewhere rural with few therapy options, this removes real barriers to care. Video sessions work just as well as in-person therapy for most people—decades of research backs that up.

But here's where honesty matters: BetterHelp is a business, and some design choices reflect that. Their therapists are independent contractors, which means variable quality. Some are exceptional; others are just getting by. The matching algorithm sometimes misses the mark. And unlike traditional therapy, there's no ongoing care coordination if you have complex needs—you're essentially hiring a solo practitioner, not accessing a system. Also, if you're in acute crisis, BetterHelp isn't the answer. They're built for ongoing support, not emergency response.

What helps

Online therapy through BetterHelp works because it meets you where you actually are—emotionally and logistically. You get a licensed therapist, real accountability, and the flexibility to fit mental health into your real life. But it works best if you're ready to show up honestly and do the work.

Questions people ask before starting

Is BetterHelp therapy actually real therapy?
Yes. BetterHelp therapists are licensed mental health professionals (LMHCs, LCSWs, PhDs, etc.) who follow the same ethical codes as in-person therapists. The research shows online therapy is just as effective for most mental health concerns. The only difference is the medium—video, phone, or messaging instead of sitting in an office.
How much does BetterHelp cost, and is it worth it?
Plans range from about $65–90 per week for weekly therapy, depending on your location and therapist. That's roughly $260–360 per month. New members get 20% off their first month. Compare that to traditional therapy at $150–300 per session with often-longer wait times, and it's competitive. Whether it's 'worth it' depends on whether you'll actually use it.
What if I get matched with a therapist I don't click with?
You can switch therapists anytime, free, no questions asked. Finding the right fit sometimes takes one or two tries. This is actually an advantage over traditional therapy, where switching providers is harder. Give a new therapist 2-3 sessions to settle, but if it's not working, move on.
Will it actually help, or am I just paying to vent to a stranger?
It helps if you're honest and ready to work. Your therapist will listen, yes, but they'll also challenge you, assign reflections or behavioral shifts, and hold you accountable. Venting alone doesn't create change—but venting with a trained guide who helps you see patterns and try new things does. You have to be willing to be vulnerable and try.
What if I'm in crisis or having thoughts of suicide?
BetterHelp is not for active crisis. Call or text the 988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline (call or text 988), go to an ER, or call 911. BetterHelp is for ongoing mental health support, not emergency response. Once you're stable, therapy can absolutely help prevent future crises.
If you are in crisis or having thoughts of harming yourself, call or text 988 immediately — the Suicide and Crisis Lifeline, available 24 hours a day in English and Spanish. BetterHelp is not a crisis service.
