When everything feels like too much

You're doing the math constantly. How much space you take up. How many times you've texted first and wondered if they actually want to hear from you. Whether you're the friend people tolerate rather than choose. The running scorecard of every time you needed something, asked for help, or just existed in someone's day—and whether you've paid that back yet. It's exhausting being you.

This isn't vanity or low self-esteem in the way people usually talk about it. It's a deeper conviction: that your presence costs people something they can't afford to pay. That you're the heavy thing in the room. That love, friendship, family connection—these are finite resources, and you're consuming more than your share.

I keep thinking about how much easier everyone's life would be if I wasn't here. Not that I want to hurt myself—I just feel like I'm always in the way.

What makes this so painful is that it *feels* like evidence, not belief. You notice when someone takes longer to text back. You see the flash of hesitation when you ask for something small. You remember the friend who drifted away. Your brain collects these moments like proof. But what you're not seeing is the full picture: the times people light up when they see you, the help they've actually wanted to give, the ways you matter in their lives that you've completely overlooked because you're too busy keeping score of your debts.

Why this belief sticks, and how it can shift

Feeling like a burden often grows from somewhere real—maybe you went through a time when you needed a lot, and someone made you feel guilty. Maybe you grew up in a house where your needs weren't just unmet but unwelcome. Maybe you're dealing with depression or anxiety right now, and it's hard to see yourself clearly. These aren't character flaws. They're understandable responses to real pain. But they've calcified into a story you tell yourself about who you are.

The good news is that this story can change. Not overnight, and not through positive thinking alone. It changes when you have someone in your corner—a therapist who can help you see the distortion in your thinking without judgment, who can help you understand where this belief came from, and who can sit with you while you practice believing something different. Therapy for this isn't about forcing yourself to feel grateful or special. It's about untangling the lies from the truth, one session at a time.

What helps

Online therapy gives you a safe space to examine this belief in detail—where it came from, how it shows up, and what's actually true versus what you're assuming. A therapist can help you practice talking to yourself differently and test out whether people actually pull away when you're honest about needing support. Many people find that this specific struggle shifts faster than they expected, sometimes in just a few weeks.

Questions people ask before starting

If I tell a therapist I feel like a burden, won't they try to convince me I'm not?
A good therapist won't dismiss your feelings or offer empty reassurance. Instead, they'll help you examine where this belief comes from and what evidence you're actually looking at. They'll ask questions that help you see the full picture, not just the moments that confirm your fear.
I'm worried that if I start therapy, I'm proving I'm too broken for anyone to handle.
Actually, seeking help is the opposite. It shows you're willing to do the hard work to feel better—and that you respect the people in your life enough to get support rather than dumping everything on them. Therapy is a sign of strength, not weakness.
How much does therapy cost, and can I actually afford it?
Online therapy through BetterHelp typically costs around $65-90 per week for unlimited messaging and weekly sessions, which many people find more manageable than traditional therapy. First-time members get 20% off their first month, and many insurance plans cover a portion of the cost.
What if talking about this just makes me feel worse?
It's common to feel tender when you first start exploring painful beliefs—but that's different from getting worse. A skilled therapist knows how to go at a pace that feels safe for you, building understanding gradually so you're not overwhelmed. Most people feel some relief just from being heard.
What if I don't click with my first therapist?
You can switch to a different therapist anytime at no extra cost. Finding the right fit matters, and BetterHelp makes it easy to try a new match without guilt or hassle. Many people find their person on the second or third try.
If you are in crisis or having thoughts of harming yourself, call or text 988 immediately — the Suicide and Crisis Lifeline, available 24 hours a day in English and Spanish. BetterHelp is not a crisis service.
