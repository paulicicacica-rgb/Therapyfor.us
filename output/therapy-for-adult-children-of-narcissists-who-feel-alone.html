The Silence That Follows You Into Adulthood

You spent your childhood learning a specific language: the language of someone else's moods. You became an expert at reading the room, at shrinking when needed, at knowing exactly what would keep the peace. Your own feelings became background noise. Your needs were inconveniences. Love came with conditions, and you learned to earn it by being useful, by being small, by being whatever they needed you to be.

Now you're an adult, and that training doesn't just disappear. You find yourself in relationships—friendships, romantic, professional—and you're still performing. Still reading the room. Still believing that your thoughts don't matter much, that speaking up might cost you something precious. And the loneliness is particular. It's not that you lack people around you. It's that the people around you don't know the real you. They've never met her. Sometimes you're not sure you remember her either.

I realized I didn't actually know what I wanted anymore. I'd spent so long being what everyone else needed that I'd disappeared.

This kind of isolation is different from just being alone. It's the isolation of having spent decades learning that your interior world—your dreams, your fears, your preferences—was a burden on someone you depended on. It's the specific pain of realizing that you may have friendships but no real intimacy, relationships but no genuine reciprocity. You've mastered the art of being present while feeling invisible. And you're exhausted.

Why This Pattern Runs So Deep—And Why It Can Change

Therapy for adult children of narcissists isn't about "getting over" what happened. It's about finally understanding why you built your life this way, and discovering that you can rebuild it differently. The patterns we learn young run deep because they kept us safe once. They were survival. But they're survival strategies in a childhood that's long over, and now they're keeping you trapped in isolation even when safety isn't at stake anymore.

The good news is that these patterns can shift. With the right support, you can slowly learn to trust that your voice matters. That your needs aren't selfish. That showing your real self doesn't have to destroy the people you care about. Many adults who've had this experience find that therapy gives them permission to exist fully, perhaps for the first time in their lives. Not overnight. Not perfectly. But genuinely.

What helps

A skilled therapist can help you name what happened, understand how it shaped you, and practice being yourself in relationships—small steps toward reclaiming the parts of you that got buried long ago. Online therapy makes this work accessible on your schedule, in a space where you control the environment and your own comfort.

Questions people ask before starting

Won't therapy just make me focus too much on the past and what my parent did wrong?
A good therapist won't get stuck in blame. The focus is on understanding how your past shaped you, then building the skills and self-awareness to live differently now. It's less about what happened and more about what happens next in your life.
I've been fine this whole time. Do I really need help just because I had a difficult parent?
You might be managing well on the surface, but many adult children of narcissists find that "being fine" actually means being numb, isolated, or disconnected from themselves. Therapy isn't about crisis—it's about thriving instead of just surviving.
How much does this cost, and can I do it weekly without breaking the bank?
BetterHelp therapists typically cost $60–$90 per week, and new members get 20% off their first month. Weekly sessions are standard and manageable for most people. You're investing in your own life, not a luxury.
What if therapy doesn't actually help me change these patterns?
Change is gradual, but it's real. You'll likely notice shifts in how you think about yourself and your relationships within weeks. If you're not seeing any movement after a few months, that's information—and you can talk openly with your therapist or try someone new.
What if I start working with a therapist and realize they're not a good fit?
You can switch to a different therapist anytime, at no penalty. Finding the right fit matters, and BetterHelp makes it easy to change if the relationship isn't working.
