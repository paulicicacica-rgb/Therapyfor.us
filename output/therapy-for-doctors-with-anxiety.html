You Know How to Heal Others. But Your Anxiety Won't Wait for a Breakthrough.

You've trained yourself to compartmentalize. To stay calm in the moment. To make the right call when everything feels like it's falling apart. And you're good at it—so good that the people around you probably have no idea that anxiety is running a parallel track in your mind, whispering doubts, stealing sleep, tightening your chest before rounds.

The thing about being a doctor with anxiety is that you understand the physiology. You can name the neurotransmitters. You can explain what's happening in your body—and somehow that makes it worse, because understanding doesn't make it stop. You're exhausted from managing both the job and the anxiety about managing the job, and there's no one in your life who quite gets what it feels like to carry that particular weight.

I could diagnose every patient but couldn't admit I was drowning. I kept thinking if I just worked harder, stayed more focused, it would pass. It didn't. Not until I got help.

The emotional toll accumulates quietly. A patient death that hits harder than expected. A mistake that loops in your mind at 3 a.m. The pressure to be invulnerable, to never show cracks, to keep absorbing loss and uncertainty. And underneath it all, the anxiety hums—sometimes low, sometimes loud enough that you wonder how you're still functioning. But you do function. You always do. And that's exactly why you haven't stopped to address it yet.

Why This Struggle Is Different—And Why Therapy Actually Works for Doctors

Anxiety in medicine isn't weakness. It's a signal that the system you've built to survive your profession has been running on overdrive. Your hypervigilance, your perfectionism, your need to control outcomes—these protected you through med school and residency. But they're also exhausting to maintain indefinitely. Therapy doesn't ask you to become less dedicated or to care less about your patients. It asks you to learn how to process the weight instead of just carrying it.

Many doctors hesitate because they're used to being the expert in the room. Therapy flips that dynamic in a way that can feel vulnerable at first. But that's also where the real work happens. A therapist who understands the culture of medicine—the pressure, the perfectionism, the imposter syndrome that never quite goes away—can help you untangle anxiety from identity. You can still be an excellent physician and also be someone who struggles sometimes. Both things can be true.

What helps

Therapy gives you a space where you don't have to have all the answers. Through evidence-based approaches like cognitive behavioral therapy, you can identify what anxiety is costing you and build practical strategies to reclaim your mental space. Many doctors find that addressing anxiety actually improves their clinical decision-making and presence with patients.

Questions people ask before starting

Won't therapy just tell me to 'take a break' or 'stress less'? That's not realistic in medicine.
A good therapist knows medicine isn't a 9-to-5 job. They won't ask you to abandon your calling. Instead, they help you build resilience and processing skills so you can stay in the work without the anxiety running your life. It's about sustainable practice, not escape.
What if someone finds out I'm in therapy? Will it affect my career?
Privacy is protected between you and your therapist. With online therapy, sessions happen on your schedule, in your space. No one needs to know unless you tell them. Many physicians quietly work with therapists without it affecting their reputation or licensing.
How much does this cost, and will my insurance cover it?
BetterHelp offers weekly sessions starting around $60-100 per week depending on your therapist. We're currently offering 20% off your first month. Many insurance plans do reimburse for teletherapy, and we can help you navigate that. You can also try a few sessions to see if it's right for you.
I've tried therapy before and it didn't help. Why would it be different this time?
The relationship with your therapist matters enormously. If the fit wasn't right before, that's worth understanding. With online therapy, you can try a different approach, different therapist, or different modality. Sometimes it's about finding the right match and the right time in your life.
What if I get a therapist and realize they're not the right fit?
You can switch therapists anytime, at no cost or penalty. There's no contract. Finding the right person is part of the process, and it's completely okay to keep looking until you find someone who feels like the right match for you.
