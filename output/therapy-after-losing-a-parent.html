The Specific Kind of Pain That Comes With This Loss

There's a silence that follows. Not the quiet kind—the kind where the phone will never ring with their voice, where you reach for advice that will never come, where holidays become reminders of the empty chair. Losing a parent isn't just losing a person. It's losing your witness. The one person who knew your beginning and saw you become who you are. That particular grief doesn't fit neatly into the stages you read about.

And then there's the exhaustion of trying to seem fine. Of fielding condolences while internally screaming. Of the moments when you forget they're gone—a split second of relief before remembering crashes in again. The guilt of laughing at something. The anger that they left during a season when you needed them most. The fear that the pain will never soften, that you're forgetting them when memories fade, that you didn't say everything that mattered.

I thought grief meant I'd be sad forever. What I didn't expect was how lonely it would feel to be the person making decisions alone, or how much I'd need someone to just sit with me and not fix it.

Your relationship with that parent shaped you in ways you're still discovering—the way you handle conflict, whether you believe you're worthy, how you love others. Their absence doesn't just change your present. It shifts how you see your past and your future. That's not weakness. That's the actual weight of losing someone who made you who you are.

Why This Grief Needs More Than Time

Time doesn't heal grief—it just teaches you to live differently with it. And some kinds of grief get stuck. You might find yourself unable to sort through their belongings, or unable to stop sorting through them obsessively. You might swing between numbness and explosive anger. You might be fine until a song plays or you smell something familiar, and then you're back on the floor. These aren't signs you're doing grief wrong. They're signs you need someone trained to help you find your way through it, not around it.

A therapist who understands parental loss doesn't push you toward "closure"—a word that feels like a lie anyway. Instead, they help you understand where the grief lives in your body, what guilt you're carrying that isn't yours to carry, how to honor your parent while also rebuilding your identity as someone who exists in the world without them. They help you remember them without being consumed by them. That's actually possible. And it's why people reach out for help.

What helps

Therapy after losing a parent helps you process not just the loss itself, but your changing relationship with identity, responsibility, and your own future. Many people find that talking to a trained therapist—especially one experienced with grief—lets them move forward without feeling like they're abandoning their parent's memory.

Questions people ask before starting

I'm worried therapy will make me cry more, or make it worse.
Therapy doesn't create new pain—it helps you process the pain that's already there. Yes, you might cry. But that release often feels relieving, not damaging. Most people find they cry less in their daily life once they have a space to actually feel it.
Isn't it too soon? Shouldn't I just give it time?
There's no timeline for grief. Some people benefit from talking early, others after months. The right time is when you feel stuck, overwhelmed, or like you're pretending to be fine. Waiting doesn't usually make grief easier—it sometimes just makes it quieter and lonelier.
How much does this cost, and can I do it around my schedule?
Online therapy through BetterHelp starts at around $60-90 per week, and you can schedule sessions that fit your life—evenings, weekends, whenever. New members get 20% off their first month. You meet with a real, licensed therapist in a private video or chat space, no waiting room.
Will therapy actually help, or will I just be paying to talk about my problems?
The difference is that a trained therapist helps you understand patterns, process guilt, rebuild identity, and move forward in ways talking to friends can't. They have tools and experience specifically for grief. People don't just feel heard—they actually begin to feel different.
What if I don't click with my therapist?
You can switch to a different therapist anytime at no extra cost. Finding the right fit matters—grief is too important to spend it with someone who doesn't feel like a good match. BetterHelp makes it easy to try again.
