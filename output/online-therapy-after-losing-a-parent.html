The Specific Pain of Losing a Parent

You've lost more than a person. You've lost someone who shaped how you see yourself, how you move through the world, how you understand love and safety. Whether your parent died suddenly or after a long illness, whether your relationship was close or complicated, the absence is real and it's heavy. Some days it hits you in the grocery store. Some days it's the silence when you want to call them. Some days it's the realization that they'll never meet your future, never see who you're becoming.

Grief after losing a parent isn't something you 'get over.' It's something you learn to carry—and right now, that weight might feel impossible. You might be struggling to show up for work. You might feel numb one moment and shattered the next. You might be angry at them for leaving, then guilty for being angry. You might be the one holding your family together while falling apart inside. None of this is weakness. This is what losing a parent actually looks like.

I thought I was supposed to be fine by now. Three months later, I realized I wasn't fine—and that was okay. Talking to someone who wasn't family or my best friend changed everything. She helped me understand that grief doesn't have an expiration date.

What you're feeling is legitimate. The disorientation, the unfair anger, the unexpected moments when their absence hits fresh—it's all part of this. You don't need someone to tell you it will be fine or that they're in a better place. You need someone to sit with you in the reality of missing them, and help you figure out how to rebuild when a core piece of your life is gone.

Why This Grief Is So Complex—And Why Talking Helps

Losing a parent untangles threads you didn't even know were connected. Your identity shifts. Your sense of family changes. Your future looks different than you thought it would. If you're an only child, you might feel like the last surviving link to your family's history. If you have siblings, you might be processing your grief completely differently than they are, which can feel isolating. If your parent was difficult or estranged, you might feel conflicted grief—sadness mixed with relief or anger. If your parent was your foundation, you might be questioning who you are without them. Therapy isn't about moving on. It's about moving forward with someone trained to help you understand what you've lost and who you're becoming in its wake.

A therapist who specializes in grief doesn't need you to be okay. They won't push you toward closure or suggest you should be stronger by now. They'll help you name what you're experiencing, sort through the complicated feelings, and slowly rebuild your capacity to experience joy again without guilt. They'll help you honor your parent's memory in a way that feels true to you, not in the way you think you should. That kind of space—judgment-free, patient, consistent—can be the difference between drowning in this and learning to breathe.

What helps

Research shows that therapy after major loss helps people process grief more fully, reduce complicated mourning patterns, and rebuild meaning in their lives. An online therapist can meet you on your timeline, in your space, without the pressure of travel or scheduling around others. Many people find it easier to open up about parent loss when they're in a comfortable, private setting.

Questions people ask before starting

Isn't therapy just talking about sad things over and over?
Not at all. A therapist helps you process grief in a structured way—understanding your emotions, identifying what you need, and building tools to move through this. You're not rehashing pain; you're learning from it and gradually rebuilding your life. Many people are surprised by how hopeful therapy becomes over time.
I feel like I'm supposed to be fine by now. Is it too late to start therapy?
Grief has no expiration date, and there's no 'right time' to seek help. Whether it's been three weeks or three years, therapy can help you process what you've been carrying. Starting now means you don't have to white-knuckle through this alone anymore.
How much does online therapy cost, and will it fit my budget?
Sessions typically run $65–$100 per week depending on your therapist, with flexible scheduling that works around your life. BetterHelp is offering 20% off your first month, which makes starting more accessible. Most people find that the cost is worth the relief and clarity they gain.
Will talking to someone online really help with something this painful?
Yes. Research consistently shows that therapy—whether in-person or online—is highly effective for grief. Many people actually prefer online therapy for parent loss because it feels safer, more private, and easier to access from home during their hardest moments.
What if I don't click with my therapist?
You can switch anytime, for free. Finding the right fit matters, and there's no penalty for changing. Most people try 1-3 therapists before landing on someone who feels right. Your comfort and trust are non-negotiable.
