The Work You Do to Avoid Living

You know the feeling: the moment you're not working, something rises up. Anxiety. Loneliness. Grief. So you don't stop. You answer emails at midnight. You take on extra projects. You find reasons to stay late, stay busy, stay numb. Work isn't really about ambition anymore—it's become your armor.

The problem is an armor weighs a lot. Your relationships feel hollow because you're not fully there. Your body aches from exhaustion you've learned to ignore. And deep down, you suspect that if you ever really stopped, you'd have to feel everything you've been running from. So you don't stop.

I realized I wasn't afraid of failure at work—I was terrified of being alone with my thoughts.

This pattern usually started somewhere real. Maybe it was the only way to feel valued in your family. Maybe productivity became your identity after loss. Maybe chaos at home made work the only predictable thing. Whatever the root, you learned that staying busy equals staying safe. But safety built on avoidance isn't really safety. It's exhaustion pretending to be purpose.

Why This Trap Is So Hard to Break—And Why Therapy Changes It

Workaholism works. That's the cruel part. It genuinely suppresses uncomfortable feelings, gives you a sense of control, earns you validation. So your brain keeps pushing you back to work whenever something painful surfaces. Breaking this cycle isn't about willpower or knowing you should take a vacation. It's about understanding what you're actually avoiding—and learning to feel it without falling apart.

Therapy gives you a space where you don't have to stay productive to matter. A therapist helps you trace the pattern back to its source, name what you're running from, and slowly, safely, build a life where you're present for the people you love and kind to yourself. You don't have to quit your job or stop caring about your work. You just get to stop using it as anesthesia.

What helps

A therapist experienced with workaholism knows how to help you sit with discomfort without judgment—and teach you that rest isn't laziness, it's healing. Online therapy means you can find someone who gets this, without adding another errand to your impossible schedule.

Questions people ask before starting

Won't therapy just tell me to work less and relax more?
No. A good therapist won't shame your work ethic or tell you to quit. They help you understand what emotion you're avoiding and why, then build real skills to handle it. The goal isn't forced relaxation—it's freedom to choose when you work and when you rest.
I don't have time for therapy. I'm too busy.
That's actually the exact moment therapy matters most. Online therapy works around your schedule—sessions happen at home, no commute. Many people find that 50 minutes a week to address this saves them thousands of hours of burnout down the road.
How much does this cost?
BetterHelp therapists start at around $65-90 per week depending on your plan. Most people pay less than a single work lunch. Plus, you get 20% off your first month, and you can message your therapist between sessions when you need support.
Will therapy actually help if I've been like this for years?
Yes. Patterns that run deep can absolutely shift with the right support. Therapy works best when you're ready to look at what's underneath the busyness. If that's you now, the length of time you've been running actually works in your favor—you have a lot of insight to uncover.
What if I don't click with my therapist?
You can switch anytime, free of charge. Finding the right fit matters. BetterHelp makes it easy to try someone new if the first match isn't right. Most people find their person within the first few tries.
If you are in crisis or having thoughts of harming yourself, call or text 988 immediately — the Suicide and Crisis Lifeline, available 24 hours a day in English and Spanish. BetterHelp is not a crisis service.
