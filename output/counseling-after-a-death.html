The weight of losing someone

Right now, you might be moving through your day like you're underwater. You make coffee. You answer texts. But nothing tastes right. Nothing feels real. The person who died is everywhere and nowhere at once—in the empty chair at the table, in a song that suddenly breaks you, in the moment you forget they're gone and reach for your phone to call them. That small death happens again and again.

Grief isn't sadness with an endpoint. It's a complicated tangle of rage, guilt, relief, emptiness, and love all at once. Some days it's suffocating. Some days it's just a dull ache you've learned to live with. And some days you wonder if you're grieving wrong, if you should be further along, if something is broken inside you that can't be fixed.

I thought I was losing my mind. But talking to someone who actually listened—who didn't try to fix me or tell me when I should be over it—gave me permission to just break.

Here's what matters: there is no right way to grieve. Your pain isn't weakness. Your anger isn't darkness. The fact that you're still here, still trying, still looking for a way through—that takes real strength.

Why grief feels so isolating, and how talking really helps

Grief has a way of making you feel like you're the only one who understands what you've lost. Even when people around you care, they often don't know what to say. They offer platitudes. They move on faster than you can. You might hide how bad it really is because you don't want to burden anyone, so you end up carrying it alone in the quiet hours. That silence can make everything feel heavier.

A therapist trained in grief doesn't try to cheer you up or rush you forward. They sit with you in the weight of it. They help you untangle the guilt from the sadness, the regrets from the love. They give you language for what you're feeling and space to feel it without judgment. Over time, that changes something. Not the fact that they're gone, but your relationship with the loss. How you carry it. What you do with it.

What helps

Grief counseling works because it's not about forgetting or moving on. It's about learning to live alongside loss—to honor what you had while slowly finding solid ground again. Many people find that talking to a professional helps them stop feeling lost and start feeling like themselves again, even in their new reality.

Questions people ask before starting

Is it too soon to get therapy for grief? Shouldn't I just wait?
There's no timeline. Some people find it helpful to talk to someone right away; others wait a few months. Either way works. A therapist can help you process the initial shock and prevent grief from becoming isolating depression. Getting support early doesn't mean you're not grieving—it means you're taking care of yourself while you grieve.
I feel like I should be able to handle this on my own. Isn't therapy for people who are weak?
Grief isn't something you just handle—it's something you move through, and it's actually harder to do alone. Talking to a professional isn't weakness; it's the same as calling an electrician when your house is broken. You wouldn't wire it yourself. Your mind and heart deserve that kind of expert care too.
What does it actually cost, and can I afford it?
Most of our therapy matches start at just $60-90 per week depending on your plan, and you get 20% off your first month. Many people find this less than they'd spend on other self-care. Plus, your first session is usually the easiest to afford, and you can pause anytime if you need to.
Will talking about it actually help, or will it just make me cry more?
Yes, you might cry. That's not a bad thing—it often means you're finally letting the grief move instead of staying stuck inside you. And talking about it with someone who won't look away, won't judge, won't change the subject—that's healing. The tears are part of how you let go.
What if I get matched with a therapist I don't click with?
You can switch to someone else anytime, at no extra cost. Finding the right fit matters, especially with something this deep. We make it easy to try again until you find someone who feels right.
