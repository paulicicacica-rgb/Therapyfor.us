The invisible weight of being the reliable one

You were taught that excellence is non-negotiable. Precision matters. A measurement of 0.02mm off is a failure, not a rounding error. So when you landed in America—on an H1B, maybe with a green card pending—you brought that standard with you. You expect it of yourself. Your employer expects it of you. And somewhere between meeting those expectations and watching people around you succeed through networking and visibility instead of output, something started to feel wrong.

The pressure isn't just about your job. It's about your status. Your visa depends on your employer. Your career depends on not making mistakes. Your future depends on being so valuable that they can't let you go. That's not stress. That's a specific, calculated kind of fear that lives in your chest every time your manager asks for a meeting, or a project changes direction mid-stream, or someone else gets promoted for reasons you can't quite measure.

I kept thinking if I just worked harder, stayed later, caught every possible error before it happened, I'd finally feel secure. Instead I felt like I was drowning while everyone else was swimming.

Add to that the cultural whiplash. You value directness; here it's seen as cold. You plan methodically; here people celebrate pivoting and 'failing fast.' You believe in earning respect through competence; here you watch colleagues get ahead through self-promotion. The system you were trained for and the system you're living in don't match. And because you're good at what you do, you're trying to succeed in both at once. That's not sustainable. It's not even possible. But you keep trying anyway.

Why this pressure doesn't get better on its own

Here's what won't work: pushing harder. You already know that path. You've walked it. The German engineering mindset that got you here—the discipline, the refusal to accept mediocrity, the belief that enough effort solves everything—that mindset is partly why you're struggling. Because some problems can't be optimized. Some stress comes from systems outside your control. Some anxiety won't respond to a better spreadsheet or a tighter process. And the longer you treat a human problem like an engineering problem, the more alone you feel.

Therapy for your specific situation isn't about lowering your standards or becoming 'American.' It's about building resilience in a genuinely difficult situation. It's about understanding why you feel responsible for things you can't control. It's about separating your worth as a person from your performance at work. And it's about figuring out how to exist in two different systems without losing yourself in either one. A therapist who understands high-achieving, precision-oriented people—and who understands the immigrant experience—can help you find that balance without asking you to compromise who you are.

What helps

Therapy has shown measurable benefit for visa-sponsored professionals managing performance anxiety and cultural adjustment. Talking through the specific stress of your situation—with someone who won't judge you for needing to feel secure, or for struggling with American workplace norms—often helps you feel less isolated and more able to make decisions from clarity instead of fear.

Questions people ask before starting

Won't a therapist just tell me to 'relax' or be less German about things?
No. Good therapy doesn't ask you to change who you are. It helps you understand why certain things trigger such intense stress, and builds tools for managing that stress without abandoning your values. A therapist who specializes in working with high-achievers and professionals understands that precision and excellence aren't the problem—the problem is when those standards become a source of shame instead of pride.
I don't have time for therapy. I'm working 60+ hours most weeks.
That's exactly why therapy helps. One session a week—even 45 minutes—creates space to step back and think clearly about what's driving those 60-hour weeks. Many engineers find they actually work more efficiently after therapy, not less. Plus, sessions are completely flexible and happen online, so you can do them at times that fit your schedule.
How much does this cost, and will it actually help with my visa anxiety?
Online therapy through BetterHelp costs around $260–$360 per week for weekly sessions, with a 20% discount on your first month. It absolutely helps with visa anxiety. Therapists can help you separate real risks from catastrophic thinking, create a plan for job security that's actually achievable, and build confidence in your ability to navigate uncertainty. That kind of shift often reduces the physical stress symptoms—sleep issues, chest tightness, constant worry—that come with visa-sponsored status.
What if I try it and it doesn't work?
Therapy isn't magic. But it does work better when you find the right therapist. With BetterHelp, you can switch therapists anytime, at no extra cost. If someone isn't the right fit after a few sessions, you can request someone else. Most people see real shifts in their thinking and stress levels within 4–6 weeks of consistent weekly sessions.
Won't telling a therapist about my work stress put my job at risk?
No. Therapy is completely confidential—therapists are legally bound to keep what you say private. The only exceptions are if you're in immediate danger or planning to hurt someone, which doesn't apply here. Your employer will never know you're in therapy unless you tell them. This is a space that's entirely for you.
If you are in crisis or having thoughts of harming yourself, call or text 988 immediately — the Suicide and Crisis Lifeline, available 24 hours a day in English and Spanish. BetterHelp is not a crisis service.
