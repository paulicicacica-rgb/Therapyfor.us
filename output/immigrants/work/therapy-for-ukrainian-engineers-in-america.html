The weight you carry isn't about one thing

You left Ukraine—maybe by choice for opportunity, maybe because you had to. Your family is scattered across continents. You're excelling at work because that's what engineers do: solve problems, meet specs, deliver. But at night, the weight lands differently. The news. The distance. The guilt for being safe while others aren't. The surreal feeling of building a career in America while your country fights for survival.

Then there's the visa reality. H1B sponsorship. Performance that can't slip. The unspoken awareness that your job security isn't just about competence—it's about maintaining status. You're managing displacement trauma, separation anxiety, and professional pressure simultaneously. And you're probably doing it alone, because talking about feelings isn't what engineers are trained to do.

I realized I was running from the pain instead of through it. My therapist helped me see that honoring what I lost doesn't mean I can't build something here.

Grief for home doesn't expire on a timeline. Neither does the anxiety of visa uncertainty or the exhaustion of code-switching between American workplace culture and the weight you carry internally. These aren't weaknesses. They're human responses to genuinely disorienting circumstances. The fact that you're functioning—working, contributing, surviving—makes it easier to ignore how much you're actually holding. But you don't have to.

Why this specific struggle is isolating—and why therapy breaks that isolation

Most of your colleagues don't ask where you're really from or what you left behind. Your managers care about sprint velocity, not whether you slept last night or if you're thinking about your parents' safety. The engineering culture rewards efficiency and problem-solving, which is useful—until it becomes a way to suppress everything else. You've learned to compartmentalize so well that you might not even recognize when displacement fatigue is affecting your focus, your relationships, or your sense of self.

Therapy creates space for the parts of you that engineering culture doesn't have room for. A therapist who understands displacement, visa stress, and cultural transition can help you process war-related grief, homesickness, and professional pressure without judgment. They won't ask you to "just be grateful" or minimize the realness of what you've lost. Instead, they'll help you integrate your past and present—honoring where you came from while building a sustainable life here.

What helps

Therapy helps you separate the grief you're carrying from the anxiety you're manufactured. It gives you tools to manage visa-related stress, process displacement, and reconnect with yourself outside of work performance. Many Ukrainian engineers find that talking through their experience—especially with someone trained in trauma and cultural transition—lifts the invisible weight they've been carrying alone.

Questions people ask before starting

Will a therapist actually understand what it's like to be Ukrainian in America right now?
BetterHelp matches you with therapists experienced in immigration, displacement, and cultural trauma. You can view profiles, ask about their background, and switch therapists if the fit isn't right. Many of our therapists specialize specifically in work with Eastern European clients navigating visa pressure and war-related stress.
I'm worried therapy will make me feel worse, or that talking about it will distract me from work.
The opposite usually happens. Unprocessed grief and stress actually drain your focus and energy far more than processing them does. Therapy helps you compartmentalize intentionally—not by suppressing, but by creating healthy boundaries between what you carry and what you do. Most clients report better focus at work within a few weeks.
How much does this cost, and will I have time?
Sessions start at around $60-90 weekly, depending on your therapist, and we're offering 20% off your first month. Sessions are online, so you schedule around your time—early morning, lunch break, late evening. Many Ukrainian engineers fit therapy into their routine without it feeling like another obligation.
What if talking to someone doesn't actually help? What if I'm just broken from everything that's happened?
You're not broken—you're adapted. Your nervous system learned to function in crisis mode, and that served you. But crisis mode was meant to be temporary. Therapy helps recalibrate what safety feels like so you can actually rest. If therapy isn't working after a few sessions, you adjust the approach or try a different therapist at no penalty.
Can I switch therapists if the first one isn't right?
Absolutely, and it's free. Finding the right fit matters. You might want someone who gets engineering culture, or someone who speaks your language, or someone with specific experience in visa-related anxiety. You can request a new match anytime, no questions asked.
If you are in crisis or having thoughts of harming yourself, call or text 988 immediately — the Suicide and Crisis Lifeline, available 24 hours a day in English and Spanish. BetterHelp is not a crisis service.
