The weight you carry isn't just today's exhaustion

Your family survived something most Americans can't imagine. Your parents or grandparents rebuilt from rubble. You grew up with that resilience—but also with the quiet panic, the way certain sounds take you back, the stories that never quite get told but live in the room anyway. Now you're working 12-hour shifts in a kitchen where nobody knows any of this. You're good at what you do. You show up. You send money. But inside, there's a pressure that doesn't have a name.

The restaurant work itself is relentless. Low pay. Your feet hurt. The schedule doesn't match your family's needs. You're tired in a way that sleep doesn't fix. And underneath it all—the unprocessed loss, the displacement your family lived through, the guilt of having it easier than they did, the fear that if you slow down, everything falls apart—it's all still there, wearing you down one shift at a time.

I didn't realize I was angry at my parents for surviving. Therapy helped me see that the exhaustion I feel isn't just about the job—it's about carrying their trauma like it's my job to fix it.

This isn't weakness. This is the honest cost of resilience. Your body remembers what your mind tries to forget. Hypervigilance isn't a flaw—it kept your family alive. But it's also keeping you from rest, from joy, from feeling like you can ever really relax. And that matters. Your wellbeing matters, not because you've earned it by suffering enough, but because you deserve to breathe.

Why this specific struggle is so hard—and why therapy actually helps

Bosnian culture values strength, family loyalty, and forward motion. You don't complain. You work. You protect your parents from worrying about you. But that same survival mechanism that saved your family is now trapping you in a cycle of exhaustion and unprocessed grief. Therapy isn't about abandoning those values—it's about honoring yourself the same way you honor your family's survival. It's about building a life where you're not just surviving, but living.

A therapist who understands trauma, immigration, and the specific weight of your background can help you separate what's yours to carry from what belongs to history. They can help you process the inherited pain without being consumed by it. They can help you build a future that isn't just a replica of suffering, but something different. Something for you.

What helps

Therapy for trauma, grief, and burnout works. When you talk to someone trained in both cultural trauma and everyday exhaustion, you can begin to untangle what belongs to your family's history from what's happening right now. You can learn to be resilient without disappearing into work. You can heal.

Questions people ask before starting

Will a therapist from my culture be available?
BetterHelp connects you with licensed therapists, and you can specifically request someone familiar with Bosnian or Eastern European background, immigration, or trauma. If the first match isn't right, you can switch anytime at no cost.
I don't have time for therapy—I work six days a week.
Sessions happen online, on your schedule. Some people do evening appointments after their shift, or a lunch break slot. You control when and where. It's designed to fit into real life, not add another thing you have to drive to.
How much does it cost?
Plans start at around $65-90 per week for unlimited messaging and weekly video sessions. New members get 20% off their first month. Many therapists offer sliding scale if cost is a barrier. It's less than most co-pays.
Will therapy actually change anything, or is it just talking?
Therapy rewires how your nervous system responds to stress and trauma. It's not magic, but it's not just talk either. People report better sleep, less anxiety, more focus, and the ability to enjoy things again. The science is there, and the results show up in your daily life.
What if I don't feel comfortable with my therapist?
You can switch therapists anytime, for free, no questions asked. Finding the right fit matters. It might take a session or two, or you might know right away. You have total control.
If you are in crisis or having thoughts of harming yourself, call or text 988 immediately — the Suicide and Crisis Lifeline, available 24 hours a day in English and Spanish. BetterHelp is not a crisis service.
