The Expat Loneliness No One Talks About

There's a specific kind of isolation that comes with living abroad. You're surrounded by people—at work, in cafes, online—yet you feel invisible in a way that's hard to explain to people back home. They see your Instagram photos and assume you're living your best life. What they don't see is the exhaustion of constantly translating not just language, but yourself. The effort of making conversation when cultural references don't land. The small moments that used to feel automatic now requiring mental energy you don't have.

And there's the identity piece. You're not quite who you were before you left. You can't fully go back. But you're not fully integrated here either. You exist in this liminal space where you don't quite belong anywhere, and the weight of that ambiguity can be crushing. It's not homesickness exactly. It's deeper—a disorientation about who you actually are when everything familiar is gone.

I realized I was performing a version of myself for everyone around me, and the real me was just... disappearing.

What makes this especially painful is the shame spiral. You chose this. You're living somewhere others dream about. So why do you feel so empty? That guilt compounds the loneliness. You blame yourself for not being grateful enough, outgoing enough, adventurous enough. But the truth is simpler: human connection takes time, cultural adjustment is hard, and losing your entire social infrastructure would affect anyone—no matter how many passport stamps they have.

Why This Hits Different—And Why Help Actually Works

Expat isolation isn't just about not having friends nearby. It's about losing the invisible scaffolding that held your identity together. Your family's expectations. Your childhood friend's laugh. The barista who knew your order. The cultural shorthand that let you be yourself without explanation. When all that's gone, you have to rebuild who you are from scratch—and you're doing it in a language and culture that may not feel natural. That's not weakness. That's a genuine psychological adjustment that deserves real support.

Therapy works for this specific struggle because a therapist can help you untangle what's temporary adjustment (and normal) from what's deeper loneliness or disconnection. They can help you build genuine connection strategies that work for where you actually are, not where you think you should be. They can also help you process the identity questions that come up when you're living between worlds. And they do all this without judgment—without the performance you might feel doing with friends who are struggling to understand why you're not just "having an adventure."

Questions people ask before starting

Will a therapist really understand what it's like to feel alone in a place where everyone else seems to be having fun?
Yes. Many therapists specialize in working with expats and understand the specific psychological dynamics of living abroad. But even if your therapist hasn't lived abroad, their job is to help you understand and work through your experience—not to have lived it themselves. What matters is their skill at listening without judgment.
Isn't therapy just for people with serious mental health issues? I just feel lonely.
Loneliness is enough. You don't need to be in crisis to benefit from therapy. In fact, working with a therapist early—when you're struggling but still functional—is often more effective. Think of it like going to a physical therapist before an injury gets worse, not after.
How much does this cost, and is it worth it when I'm already spending money on living abroad?
Most therapists on BetterHelp charge between $60–$90 per session depending on experience and availability. We offer 20% off your first month, so you can start at around $48–$72 per session. When loneliness is affecting your sleep, work performance, and overall wellbeing, weekly support usually pays for itself in clarity and resilience.
What if therapy doesn't actually help? What if I'm just meant to feel this way abroad?
That's not how this works. You're not broken or destined to be lonely. Therapy gives you tools to process grief, build real connections, and understand your identity across cultures. Most people see shifts in 4–6 weeks. And if your therapist isn't clicking, you can switch anytime—no penalty, no explanation needed.
What if I don't like my therapist or feel like they're not getting it?
You can switch therapists anytime, completely free. BetterHelp makes it easy to find someone new in minutes. The fit between you and your therapist matters, and there's no reason to stay with someone who isn't working for you. You're in control.
If you are in crisis or having thoughts of harming yourself, call or text 988 immediately — the Suicide and Crisis Lifeline, available 24 hours a day in English and Spanish. BetterHelp is not a crisis service.
