You know that feeling. The one that keeps you up at night.

A panic attack hits without warning. Your body floods with adrenaline. Your hands shake. You can't catch your breath. Your mind screams that something is dying—that you're dying—even though the rational part of you knows better. It lasts minutes but feels like hours. And when it finally passes, the exhaustion is real. But the real prison? The waiting. Wondering when it'll happen again. Avoiding places, people, situations that might trigger the next one. That anticipatory dread becomes its own kind of torture.

The worst part is that you start living in your own head. Every heartbeat feels suspicious. Every moment of dizziness sends a spike of panic through you. You research symptoms obsessively. You check your pulse. You plan escape routes. The panic attack itself might be over, but the fear of the next one has moved in and taken control of your days—your weeks, maybe your months.

I wasn't afraid of the panic attack anymore. I was afraid of being afraid. And that fear was running my entire life.

This cycle—panic, then fear of panic, then panic again—is exhausting. It can make you feel isolated and ashamed, like you should just be able to handle this on your own. But panic disorder isn't a weakness or a character flaw. It's your nervous system stuck in overdrive, and it responds to the right kind of help.

Why this trap is so hard to escape—and why therapy actually works

Your brain has learned to see danger everywhere. A fast heartbeat means danger. Shortness of breath means danger. Dizziness means danger. So it keeps triggering the fight-or-flight response, even when you're sitting safely on your couch. The more you avoid situations out of fear, the more your brain believes those situations really are dangerous. You're caught in a feedback loop that gets tighter with each cycle.

Therapy breaks this loop. A trained therapist helps you understand why your nervous system is misfiring and teaches you concrete tools to calm it down. Cognitive-behavioral therapy for panic is one of the most evidence-backed treatments we have. You learn to recognize the thoughts that fuel panic, to breathe in ways that actually settle your nervous system, and gradually—without forcing it—to retrain your brain that you're safe. The goal isn't to never feel anxious again. It's to get your life back.

What helps

Therapy for panic attacks works best when it's focused and consistent. Most people start feeling relief within 4-8 weeks of regular sessions. Your therapist will work with you at your pace, never forcing exposure before you're ready, and teaching you skills you can use anytime panic shows up.

Questions people ask before starting

What if my therapist doesn't understand how terrifying this really is?
A good therapist who specializes in panic disorder gets it immediately—they've helped dozens of people through exactly this. If you connect with someone who doesn't feel like the right fit, you can switch to a different therapist anytime at no extra cost.
Will I have to face my fears right away, even if I'm not ready?
No. Good therapy for panic is gradual and happens at your pace. Your therapist will build skills with you first—breathing techniques, grounding exercises, thought work—before any gentle exposure. You're in control of the speed.
How much does therapy cost, and can I afford to do this weekly?
Sessions are typically $60-90 per week depending on your therapist. Many people start weekly and taper down as they improve. We offer 20% off your first month, which helps take the financial pressure off right at the beginning.
Is therapy actually proven to help panic attacks, or is it just talking?
Cognitive-behavioral therapy for panic disorder has decades of research backing it. People see measurable improvement in panic frequency and intensity. It's not just talking—it's learning and practicing specific techniques that retrain your nervous system.
What if I'm afraid to even start therapy? What if I panic during a session?
Panicking during a session is actually okay—your therapist is trained for it and will help you through it in real time. Starting is the hardest part, but most people feel some relief just from being heard and having a plan.
If you are in crisis or having thoughts of harming yourself, call or text 988 immediately — the Suicide and Crisis Lifeline, available 24 hours a day in English and Spanish. BetterHelp is not a crisis service.
