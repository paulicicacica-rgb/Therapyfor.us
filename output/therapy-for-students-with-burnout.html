You're not just tired. You're depleted.

Burnout in school looks different than regular tiredness. It's the moment you realize you've stopped caring about things that used to matter to you. Classes you once found interesting now feel pointless. Social events feel exhausting instead of fun. You're scrolling through your phone at midnight, knowing you have an exam tomorrow, unable to do either—unable to sleep, unable to study, just stuck in this gray nothingness between caring and collapsing.

What makes it worse is the isolation. You watch other students seem fine, energized, like they have their lives figured out. You don't talk about how behind you feel, how much you're doubting whether this path is even right for you, how the thought of next semester makes your chest tight. So you keep it to yourself, and the weight of keeping it private becomes part of the burnout itself.

I felt like I was drowning in slow motion—everyone could see me, but nobody could tell I was going under.

The uncertainty doesn't help. You're supposed to have a plan, a direction, some sense that all this exhaustion is building toward something meaningful. But instead, you're questioning everything: whether your major makes sense, whether college is worth it, whether you're even capable of finishing. That doubt whispers underneath every assignment, every grade, every late night. And burnout doesn't let you think clearly enough to answer any of it.

Why this hits so hard—and why talking to someone actually helps

Student burnout is specific. It's not just stress or procrastination. It's what happens when high pressure meets perfectionism meets isolation meets existential doubt, all at once, for months. Your nervous system is in overdrive. Your motivation has flatlined. You might be sleeping too much or too little, eating irregularly, withdrawing from friends. The thought of asking for help feels like one more impossible thing on an already impossible list. But that's exactly when talking to a therapist breaks the cycle.

Therapy gives you a space where nobody's grading you, nobody's judging your choices, and you don't have to perform. A therapist can help you untangle what's burnout, what's anxiety about the future, what's actual depression—because they often live together. They can help you rebuild some sense of agency when everything feels like it's being done to you. And they can help you figure out what you actually want, separate from what you think you should want. That clarity alone changes everything.

What helps

Therapy for student burnout isn't about trying harder or 'powering through.' It's about understanding what's driving the exhaustion, learning how to rebuild your nervous system, and reconnecting with what makes your education (and your life) feel meaningful again. Most students see shifts in how they're feeling within the first few weeks.

Questions people ask before starting

I'm worried therapy will take time I don't have. I'm already drowning.
One 45-minute session a week is often easier to protect than you'd think—and it actually *creates* time by helping you study more efficiently and sleep better. Most students find they're more productive after therapy starts, not less.
What if I open up about my doubts and it makes things worse?
A good therapist helps you process these doubts in a way that brings clarity, not more panic. Most of the time, the fear of saying it out loud is worse than actually naming it. And naming it is the first step to dealing with it.
How much does online therapy cost for students?
BetterHelp sessions are typically $90–$120 per week, and many students qualify for discounts. First month is 20% off. Some therapists offer sliding scale rates if cost is a barrier. Many student insurance plans also cover part of it—worth checking.
Will therapy actually help, or am I just paying someone to listen?
Therapy is structured. Your therapist will help you identify patterns, teach you concrete tools to manage stress and anxiety, and help you clarify what you actually want. It's not just venting—it's changing how you think about and move through your situation.
What if my therapist doesn't get me?
You can switch to a different therapist anytime, at no penalty or extra cost. The fit matters. BetterHelp makes it easy to find someone who clicks with you.
If you are in crisis or having thoughts of harming yourself, call or text 988 immediately — the Suicide and Crisis Lifeline, available 24 hours a day in English and Spanish. BetterHelp is not a crisis service.
